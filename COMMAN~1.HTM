📚 Today's Macro Lesson

📈 CONCEPT: Yield Curve Inversion

A yield curve inverts when short-term interest rates (e.g., 2Y Treasury) exceed long-term rates (10Y Treasury). This is abnormal — normally, investors demand higher compensation for longer time horizons.

Why does it matter? An inverted curve signals that bond markets expect the central bank to cut rates in the future — which typically happens in response to economic weakness or recession. The 2Y/10Y spread has inverted before every US recession since 1955.

Current status: 2Y at 4.57%, 10Y at 4.52% = -5bps inverted. The curve is un-inverting (steepening) — historically the actual recession risk is highest after the curve re-steepens from inversion.

💸 Bond Concept of the Day

📉 Real Yields vs Nominal Yields

A nominal yield is the stated yield on a bond (e.g., 4.52% on the 10Y UST). A real yield subtracts expected inflation: Real Yield = Nominal – Breakeven Inflation.

With 10Y nominal at 4.52% and 10Y breakeven at 2.34%, the US real yield is +2.18% — genuinely restrictive. High real yields compete with equities (why hold risk assets when cash pays real returns?) and tighten financial conditions.

Watch for: If real yields fall (Fed cuts faster than inflation drops), this is historically bullish for gold, growth stocks, and risk assets.

🧠 Investing Principle of the Day

💡 Ray Dalio: The Credit Cycle

Every major economic cycle is fundamentally a credit cycle. When credit expands, spending increases, growth accelerates, and asset prices rise. When credit contracts (tightening, defaults, deleveraging), the reverse occurs.

We are currently in a late-cycle phase: credit is still available but becoming more selective. Watch credit spreads, bank lending standards, and consumer delinquencies — these are the early warning systems of the next credit contraction.

Actionable: In late cycle, shift toward higher quality (IG bonds over HY, quality equities over speculative). Reduce duration risk. Build cash reserves for opportunity deployment when the cycle turns.

🎯 Macro Concept of the Day

🔄 The Dollar Liquidity Cycle

The US dollar is the world's reserve currency — most global trade, debt, and commodity contracts are denominated in USD. When the dollar strengthens, it creates a global liquidity squeeze: countries with USD debt face higher repayment costs, commodity prices fall, and capital flees emerging markets.

Conversely, a weakening dollar (like now, DXY -2.4% YTD) loosens global liquidity: EM assets benefit, commodities rise, and global risk appetite improves. This is why gold, EM equities, and Bitcoin often rally when the dollar weakens.

The mechanism: Fed rate cuts → USD weakens → Global USD liquidity expands → Risk-on globally. This is why the Fed's every move has global consequences.

What to Study Next
📖 Duration Risk — How bond prices move with interest rates. Every 1% rate change ≈ 7-10% price move on 10Y bond.
📖 Carry Trade — Borrow in low-rate currency (JPY), invest in high-rate. Unwind = sudden yen surge + EM selloff.
📖 Reflexivity (Soros) — Market prices influence fundamentals which influence prices. Momentum is not irrational — it's reflexive.
📖 Quantitative Tightening — Fed shrinking balance sheet = reducing bank reserves = tightening liquidity. Watch repo rates for stress.
📖 Term Premium — Extra yield demanded for holding long-term bonds. Rising = fiscal fears or inflation uncertainty.
